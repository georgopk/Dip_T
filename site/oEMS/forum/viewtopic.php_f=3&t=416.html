Post by thorsten » Mon 24 Oct 2016, 19:40

Hi,

I think the RCS tutorial is what you should have a closer look at:

See this lines for example:

Code: Select all

EF = ReadUI( 'et', Sim_Path, f0 ); % time domain/freq domain voltage
Pin = 0.5*norm(E_dir)^2/Z0 .* abs(EF.FD{1}.val).^2;
The file "et" contains the excitation function (over time) which is needed to calculate the power distribution in the frequency domain.
This pretty much gives you the incident power.

Alternatively you can use "EF.FD{1}.val" to normalize you recorded field and compare it directly to E_dir. Both should work...

I hope that did help?

Post by thorsten » Thu 27 Oct 2016, 19:00

I wonder, why the size of EF.TD{1}.val is so small.
I think with small you mean short?
This value holds the times at which a field/probe was recorded. Usually not every timestep is recorded which explains why this is usually much smaller than the number of timesteps simulated). The timestep is defined by the mesh, but how densely you sample your fields/probes should depend on the highest available frequency (nyquist criteria) not the timestep required by the FDTD method.
Pin is zero (2e-24), but i should be >1.
Why is that? Please remember that looking at time-domain values and frequency domain values are two very different thinks. E_dir is the amplitude for the time-signal (Gaussian pulse). EF is the signal energy at a single frequency for that given pulse and thus a very tiny value.
You need to make sure you compare signals of the same domain (TD or FD).

Doing all this normalization correctly is no easy task and you really need to know what you are doing...
So far I'm still not quite sure what you intend to do...
Pin is certainly the incident power at a given frequency. I'm not sure what you FEF formula is supposed to calculate at the moment... I don't think it makes any sense...
You normalize the first value of the Gaussian pulse with E_dir... Why?
